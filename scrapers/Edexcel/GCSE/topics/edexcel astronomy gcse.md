Examined Content for Pearson Edexcel Level 1/Level 2 GCSE (9–1) in Statistics
1. Collection of Data
(a) Planning
1a.01: Know that a hypothesis can be tested only through the appropriate collection and analysis of data.
Specifying a hypothesis is expected, e.g., "as motorcycles get older, their value is likely to go down."
1a.02: Know the constraints in designing an investigation to test a hypothesis.
Factors include time, costs, ethical issues, confidentiality, convenience.
1a.03: Determine proactive strategies to mitigate issues during the statistical enquiry process.
Examples include dealing with difficulties in identifying the population, non-response issues, or unexpected outcomes.
(b) Types of Data
1b.01: Know and apply terms for different types of data that can be collected for statistical analysis.
Raw data
Quantitative
Qualitative
Categorical
Ordinal
Discrete
Continuous
Ungrouped
Grouped
Bivariate
Multivariate (Higher Tier only)
1b.02: Know the advantages and implications of merging data into more general categories and grouping numerical data into class intervals.
Understand class width.
Implications of grouping data (e.g., loss of accuracy in calculations and presentations).
1b.03: Know and apply the terms explanatory (independent) variables and response (dependent) variables.
Explanatory variable should be on the x-axis in scatter diagrams.
1b.04: Know the difference between primary and secondary data.
Advantages and disadvantages of each.
Consider reliability and accuracy of the data.
Recognition of possible constraints in accessing the data.
(c) Population and Sampling
1c.01: Know the difference between population, sample frame, and sample.
Identify a population.
Suggest a suitable sampling frame.
1c.02: Know that "population" can have different meanings within a stated context.
Examples: all employees in an office, all females in the UK, all items produced in a factory.
1c.03: Know reasons for employing judgment sampling or opportunity (convenience) sampling.
Associated risks of bias.
Includes cluster sampling and quota sampling.
1c.04: Know appropriate sampling techniques to avoid bias.
Random sampling
Systematic sampling
Quota sampling
1c.05: Know the key features of a simple random sample.
Techniques to select random members from a population (e.g., dice, cards, random number lists, calculator functions).
All items in the population should have the same likelihood of inclusion.
1c.06: Use stratification and know when this is appropriate before sampling.
Identify suitable strata (e.g., gender or age group).
Calculate appropriate strata sizes.
Stratifying by one category (Foundation Tier).
Stratifying by one or more categories (Higher Tier).

Experimental (laboratory, field, and natural)

1d.02: Know the importance of reliability and validity with regard to collected data.
Reliability: extent to which repeated measurements yield similar results.
Validity: extent to which a test measures what was intended.
1d.03: Determine factors that may lead to bias.
Issues of sensitivity of the content matter.
Level of control.
Minimize data distortion.

Avoiding biased sources
Time factors

Pilots for questionnaires and pre-tests for experiments.

Missing data
Incorrect formats
Non-responses
Incomplete responses
1d.06: Know why data may need to be "cleaned" before further processing.
Issues that arise on spreadsheets.
Techniques to clean data in context.
Consideration of genuine and other outliers and anomalies.
Removal of extraneous symbols or notation when using technology.
1d.07: Know the importance of identifying and controlling extraneous variables.
Use of control groups (Higher Tier only).
Advantage of using matched pairs when using control groups (Higher Tier only).

Positive skew: values above the median have a greater spread than values below the median.

By using the equation of the regression line (Higher Tier only):
Linear regression line: y = a + bx
2e.05: Apply formula to determine Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient (Higher Tier only):
Formula will be provided.
Values found using calculator functions are permissible.


2e.06: Interpret calculated or given Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient in the context of the problem (Higher Tier only):
Values range from -1 to +1.
Values closer to these limits indicate stronger correlation.
+1 means perfect agreement, -1 means complete opposite ranks, and 0 means no agreement between ranks.
2e.07: Interpret given Pearson’s product moment correlation coefficient (PMCC) in the context of the problem (Higher Tier only):
Values range from -1 to +1.
+1 means perfect linear positive correlation, -1 means perfect linear negative correlation, and 0 means no linear correlation.
2e.08: Understand the distinction between Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient and Pearson’s product moment correlation coefficient (PMCC) (Higher Tier only):
PMCC measures the strength of linear correlation.
Spearman’s rank correlation measures the strength of rank correlation.
For positive non-linear correlation, both coefficients will be positive, but Spearman’s coefficient will be greater than PMCC.

(g) Quality Assurance (Higher Tier only)
2g.01: Know that a set of sample means are more closely distributed than individual values from the same population:
Example: The set of mean heights from each class in a school will show less variation than the set of heights of all students in the school.
2g.02: Use action and warning lines in quality assurance sampling applications:
Control charts used for sample mean, median, or range.
Understand that almost all means, medians, or ranges fall inside the action lines (±3 standard deviations from the expected value).
Only 1 in 20 values fall outside the warning lines (±2 standard deviations from the expected value).
Know the action to be taken if a sample value falls outside each type of limit.
(h) Estimation
2h.01: Use calculated or given summary statistical data to make estimates of population characteristics:
Use samples to estimate population mean.
Use sample data to predict population proportions.
Example: Predict that approximately half the population will be above the sample median.
2h.02: Apply Petersen capture-recapture formula to calculate an estimate of the size of a population (Higher Tier only):
Number in population = (sample size 1 × sample size 2) / number marked in sample 2.
Know the assumptions needed and be familiar with their appropriateness in practice.
2h.03: Know that sample size has an impact on reliability and replication:
Results/conclusions are likely to be more reliable if based on larger samples.


3. Probability
3p.01: Use collected data to calculate estimates of probabilities:
Use relative frequency.
3p.02: Compare the probability of different possible outcomes using the 0–1 or 0–100% scale and statements of likelihood:
Certain
Impossible
Evens
Likely
Very unlikely
3p.03: Use probability values to calculate expected frequency of a specified characteristic within a sample or population:
Use probability as a proportion to find expected frequency.
3p.04: Use collected data and calculated probabilities to determine and interpret relative risks and absolute risks:
Express in terms of expected frequencies in groups.
3p.05: Compare experimental data with theoretical predictions to identify possible bias within the experimental design.
3p.06: Recognize that experimental probability will tend towards theoretical probability as the number of trials increases when all variables are random:
Increasing sample size generally leads to better estimates of probability and population parameters.
3p.07: Use two-way tables, sample space diagrams, tree diagrams, and Venn diagrams to represent all the different outcomes possible for at most three events:
Understand the terms mutually exclusive and exhaustive.
Know the addition law for two mutually exclusive events: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B).
Know the general addition law: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B).
3p.08: Know and apply the formal notation for independent events:
P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B)
P(A|B) = P(A)
P(B|A) = P(B)
3p.09: Know and apply the formal notation for conditional probability:
P(A|B) = P(A and B) / P(B)
3p.10: Comment on the differences between experimental and theoretical values in terms of possible bias:
Compare observed outcomes with expected frequencies from a binomial model.
3p.11: Know and interpret the characteristics of a binomial distribution (Higher Tier only):
Notation: B(n, p).
Mean of a binomial distribution: np.
Conditions for a binomial model to be suitable.
Calculate probabilities or use given probabilities (e.g., calculator functions, spreadsheets, Pascal’s triangle).
3p.12: Know and interpret the characteristics of a normal distribution (Higher Tier only):
Notation: N(µ, σ²).
Symmetrical "bell" shape.
Median, mean, and mode are equal.


3p.13: Know that, for a normal distribution (Higher Tier only):
Values more than three standard deviations from the mean are very unusual.
Approximately 95% of the data lie within two standard deviations of the mean.
Approximately 68% (just over two-thirds) lie within one standard deviation of the mean.
Be familiar with the conditions which make a normal model suitable:
Data are continuous.
The distribution is symmetrical and bell-shaped.
Mean, median, and mode are approximately equal.
