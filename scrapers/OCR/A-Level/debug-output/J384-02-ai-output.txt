1. People and Society (J384/02)
   1.1. Urban Futures
      1.1.1. Why do more than half the world’s population live in urban areas?
         1.1.1.1. How is the global pattern of urbanisation changing?
            1.1.1.1.1. How urban growth rates vary in parts of the world with contrasting levels of development.
            1.1.1.1.2. Outline characteristics of world cities and megacities and their changing distribution since 1950.
         1.1.1.2. What does rapid urbanisation mean for cities?
            1.1.1.2.1. Understand the causes of rapid urbanisation in LIDCs, including the push and pull factors of rural-urban migration and internal growth.
            1.1.1.2.2. Investigate the consequences of rapid urban growth in LIDCs.
            1.1.1.2.3. Understand the causes and consequences of contrasting urban trends in ACs, including suburbanisation, counter-urbanisation and re-urbanisation.
      1.1.2. What are the challenges and opportunities for cities today?
         1.1.2.1. What is life like for people in a city?
            1.1.2.1.1. The city’s location and importance within its region, the country, and the wider world.
            1.1.2.1.2. Patterns of national and international migration and how this is changing the growth and character of the city.
            1.1.2.1.3. Explore the ways of life in the city, such as culture, ethnicity, housing, leisure and consumption.
            1.1.2.1.4. Investigate the contemporary challenges that affect life in the AC city, such as housing availability, transport provision, access to services and inequality.
            1.1.2.1.5. Investigate the contemporary challenges that affect life in the LIDC or EDC city, such as informal settlements, informal sector jobs, health or waste disposal.
         1.1.2.2. How can cities become more sustainable?
            1.1.2.2.1. For each city investigate one initiative to make it more sustainable, such as use of brownfield sites, waste recycling and transport improvements.
   1.2. Dynamic Development
      1.2.1. Why are some countries richer than others?
         1.2.1.1. What is development and how can it be measured?
            1.2.1.1.1. Definition of ‘development’ and the ways in which countries can be classified, such as AC, EDC and LIDC.
            1.2.1.1.2. Global distribution of ACs, EDCs and LIDCs.
            1.2.1.1.3. Economic and social measures of development, such as GNI per capita and Human Development Index, and how they illustrate the consequences of uneven development.
         1.2.1.2. What has led to uneven development?
            1.2.1.2.1. Outline the human and physical factors influencing global uneven development.
            1.2.1.2.2. Explore the factors that make it hard for countries to break out of poverty, including debt, trade and political unrest.
      1.2.2. Are LIDCs likely to stay poor?
         1.2.2.1. How has an LIDC developed so far?
            1.2.2.1.1. Overview of the economic development of an LIDC, including influences of population, society, technology and politics, particularly in the past 50 years, or post-independence.
            1.2.2.1.2. Explore whether Rostow’s model can help determine the country’s path of economic development.
            1.2.2.1.3. Progress towards three relevant Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) for this LIDC.
            1.2.2.1.4. Investigate how the LIDC’s wider political, social and environmental context has affected its development.
         1.2.2.2. What global connections influence its development?
            1.2.2.2.1. The country’s international trade, such as potential reliance on a single, or few, commodities and how this influences development.
            1.2.2.2.2. The benefits and problems of trade and Trans National Company (TNC) investment for development.
            1.2.2.2.3. The advantages and disadvantages of international aid such as debt relief for its development.
         1.2.2.3. What development strategy is most appropriate?
            1.2.2.3.1. Compare the advantages and disadvantages of one top-down and one bottom-up strategy in the country.
   1.3. UK in the 21st Century
      1.3.1. How is the UK changing in the 21st century?
         1.3.1.1. What does the UK look like in the 21st century?
            1.3.1.1.1. Overview of human and physical geographical characteristics of the UK, including population density, land use, rainfall and relief, and significant issues associated with these characteristics, including water stress and housing shortages.
         1.3.1.2. How is the UK’s population changing?
            1.3.1.2.1. Overview of population trends in the UK since 2001, using population pyramids and migration statistics, to determine its position on the Demographic Transition Model.
            1.3.1.2.2. An understanding of the causes, effects, spatial distribution and responses to an ageing population.
            1.3.1.2.3. A summary of the how the population structure and ethnic diversity of a named place of the UK has changed since 2001.
         1.3.1.3. How is the UK’s economy changing?
            1.3.1.3.1. Identify major economic changes in the UK since 2001 by examining changes in the job market including political priorities, changing employment sectors and working hours.
            1.3.1.3.2. Investigate the pattern of core UK economic hubs.
            1.3.1.3.3. Identify the changes in one economic hub and its significance to its region and the UK.
      1.3.2. Is the UK losing its global significance?
         1.3.2.1. What is the UK’s political role in the world?
            1.3.2.1.1. Examine the UK’s political role in one global conflict through its participation in international organisations.
         1.3.2.2. How is the UK’s cultural influence changing?
            1.3.2.2.1. Explore the UK’s media exports and their global influence including television programmes and film.
            1.3.2.2.2. The contribution of ethnic groups to the cultural life of the UK through one of food, media or fashion.
   1.4. Resource Reliance
      1.4.1. Will we run out of natural resources?
         1.4.1.1. How has increasing demand for resources affected our planet?
            1.4.1.1.1. Outline the factors leading to demand outstripping supply of food, energy and water.
            1.4.1.1.2. Overview of how environments and ecosystems are used and modified by humans including mechanisation of farming and commercial fishing to provide food, deforestation and mining to provide energy, reservoirs and water transfer schemes to provide water.
      1.4.2. Can we feed nine billion people by 2050?
         1.4.2.1. What does it mean to be food secure?
            1.4.2.1.1. Understand the term ‘food security’ and the human and physical factors which influence this.
            1.4.2.1.2. How world patterns of access to food are illustrated, such as the world hunger index and average daily calorie consumption.
            1.4.2.1.3. Investigate the differences between Malthusian and Boserupian theories about the relationship between population and food supply.
         1.4.2.2. How can countries ensure their food security?
            1.4.2.2.1. Case study of attempts to achieve food security in one country to include investigation of statistics relating to food consumption and availability over time.
            1.4.2.2.2. The success of one attempt in helping achieve food security at a local scale such as food banks, urban gardens and allotments.
            1.4.2.2.3. The effectiveness of one past and one present attempt to achieve food security at a national scale such as global food trade, GM crops, ‘The Green Revolution’ and food production methods.
         1.4.2.3. How sustainable are these strategies?
            1.4.2.3.1. Explore the environmental, economic and social sustainability of attempts to achieve food security, in relation to ethical consumerism, such as fairly traded goods and food waste, food production, such as organic methods and intensive farming, technological developments, such as GM crops and hydroponics, small scale ‘bottom up’ approaches, such as urban gardens and permaculture.
