1. Drama and the theatre in ancient Athenian society
   1.1 role and significance of drama and the theatre in ancient Athenian society, including the religious context of the dramatic festivals
   1.2 the organisation of the City Dionysia, including the make up and involvement of the theatre audience
   1.3 structure of the theatre space, and how this developed during the 5th and 4th centuries BC including:
       1.3.1 machinery associated with the theatre; the crane and the wheel platform, and how they contributed to the staging of Greek drama
   1.4 the representation in visual and material culture of theatrical and dramatic scenes

2. Nature of tragedy
   2.1 the origins of tragedy and how it developed during the 5th century BC, including its relationship to satyr-plays
   2.2 the contributions of Aeschylus, Sophocles and Euripides
   2.3 use of actors and the Chorus
   2.4 use of masks, costumes and props
   2.5 common themes of tragedy
   2.6 the relationship between the cultural context and subject matter of the plays
   2.7 Aristotle’s theories about tragedy, including peripeteia (reversal of fortune), hamartia (tragic mistake), catharsis (purging of emotions)

3. Nature of (old) comedy
   3.1 the origins of comedy and how it developed during the 5th century BC, including the contribution of Aristophanes
   3.2 use of actors and the Chorus
   3.3 use of masks, costumes and props
   3.4 types of humour, comic techniques and effects
   3.5 the common themes of comedy
   3.6 the relationship between the cultural context and subject matter of the plays

4. Literary techniques, structure, and dramatic conventions
   4.1 plot structure
   4.2 plot devices, including messenger speeches, agon, parabasis
   4.3 use and choice of language, literary devices and descriptive techniques, including imagery and dramatic irony
   4.4 characterisation, including the role of the chorus
   4.5 styles and techniques of the different playwrights

       5.1.1 ancient religious concepts, beliefs and practices, including:
           5.1.1.1 the role of the gods
           5.1.1.2 fate and free will
           5.1.1.3 prophecy and prophets
           5.1.1.4 religious rituals and acts
       5.1.2 importance of the polis (city), including:
           5.1.2.1 position and role of men, women and slaves in society
           5.1.2.2 political ideas and ideals
       5.1.3 importance of family relationships
       5.1.4 tragic heroism, including:
           5.1.4.1 the nature of heroes and heroism
           5.1.4.2 justice and revenge
       5.1.5 possible interpretation of these themes and motifs by both ancient and modern audiences
       5.1.6 the representation of such themes and motifs in the visual/material record

8. When studying literature learners will be required to show knowledge and understanding of:
   8.1 ways in which writers shape meanings in classical texts
   8.2 ways in which classical texts might be interpreted by different readers or audiences both in an ancient and modern context
   8.3 ways in which classical texts relate to the historical, social, political, religious and cultural contexts in which they are written and received
   8.4 ways in which classical texts relate to literary traditions and genres of the classical world

9. When studying literature learners should be able to:
   9.1 respond critically to texts and consider how the attitudes and values of the classical world or author are expressed
   9.2 apply their knowledge of cultural contexts to support, substantiate and inform evidence-based judgements about the classical texts
   9.3 use classical texts to demonstrate an understanding of the social, historical and cultural context of the classical world, with recognition, where appropriate, of the complex issues of reliability and the difference between what a text might say and what can be inferred from this
   9.4 critically explore and explain the possibility of different responses to a text from different audiences, from the classical period, up until the modern day

    10.1 Theatre of Dionysus at Athens
    10.2 Theatre of Thorikos, a coastal deme of Attica
    10.3 Krater “Choregos Vase” or “Comic Angels”, choregoi with Aegisthus and Pyrrhias
    10.4 Volute krater by the “Pronomos” painter, depicting team of actors celebrating with costumes, masks, aulos player, playwright and Dionysus
    10.5 Calyx krater fragment by Capodarso painter, depicting Jocasta and Oedipus the King
    10.6 Bell krater by McDaniel Painter, “Cheiron Vase” Red-figure calyx krater depicting Medea’s escape
    10.7 Red-figure column krater in mannerist style, “Basel Dancers”
    10.8 Pelike by Phiale painter depicting tragic actors dressing
    10.9 Black-figure chous depicting two chorus-members dressed as birds, c.480 BC in British Museum (B509)
    10.10 Red-figure bell krater by Schiller Painter, depicting scene with wine-skin & boots from Women at the Thesmophoria (Thesmophoriazusae)

11. When studying visual/material culture learners will be required to show knowledge and understanding of:
    11.1 the appearance, style, content and original location (as applicable) of the sources
    11.2 what the sources can tell us about the classical world, and what they cannot
    11.3 the ways in which the social, political, religious and cultural context of production impacts on the creation of visual/material culture
    11.4 appropriate methods of analysis and interpretation, including issues of purpose, production and form
    11.5 the range of possible interpretations of visual/material culture when looked at from different perspectives, in an ancient and modern context

12. When studying visual/material culture learners should be able to:
    12.1 respond critically to artefacts, identifying different possible interpretations, taking account of issues such as those of audience and purpose
    12.2 know and understand how materials and artefacts relate to their wider context and how this social, historical, political, religious and cultural context affected their creation and interpretation
    12.3 evaluate the usefulness of visual/material culture when investigating the classical world
    12.4 apply their knowledge of cultural contexts to support, substantiate and inform their judgements
    12.5 use their understanding of visual/material culture to demonstrate an understanding of the social, historical and cultural context of the classical world with recognition, of the limitations of evidence (such as the issue of fragmentary or relocated material)
    12.6 critically explore and explain the possibility of different responses to materials from different audiences, from the classical period, up until the modern day
