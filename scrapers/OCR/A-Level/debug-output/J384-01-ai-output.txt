1. Global Hazards
   1.1. How can weather be hazardous?
      1.1.1. Why do we have weather extremes?
         1.1.1.1. Outline of the global circulation system including the effects of high and low pressure belts in creating climatic zones.
         1.1.1.2. How the global circulation of the atmosphere causes extremes in weather conditions in different parts of the world.
         1.1.1.3. The extremes in weather conditions associated with wind, temperature and precipitation in contrasting countries.
         1.1.1.4. The distribution and frequency of tropical storms and drought, and whether these have changed over time.
         1.1.1.5. Outline the causes of the extreme weather conditions associated with tropical storms.
         1.1.1.6. Outline the causes of the extreme weather conditions of El Niño/La Niña leading to drought.
      1.1.2. When does extreme weather become a hazard?
         1.1.2.1. Case studies of two contrasting natural weather hazard events arising from extreme weather conditions. The case studies must include a natural weather hazard from each bullet point below: flash flooding or tropical storms, heat wave or drought.
         1.1.2.2. There must be one UK based and one non-UK based natural weather hazard event.
         1.1.2.3. For each chosen hazard event, study the place specific causes (including the extreme weather conditions which led to the event), consequences of and responses to the hazard.
   1.2. How do plate tectonics shape our world?
      1.2.1. What processes occur at plate boundaries?
         1.2.1.1. The structure of the Earth.
         1.2.1.2. Understanding of slab pull and ridge push to show how tectonic plates move.
         1.2.1.3. The processes that take place at constructive, destructive, conservative and collision plate boundaries as well as hotspots.
         1.2.1.4. How the movement of tectonic plates causes earthquakes, including shallow and deep focus, and volcanoes, including shield and composite.
      1.2.2. How can tectonic movement be hazardous?
         1.2.2.1. A case study of a tectonic event that has been hazardous for people, including specific causes, consequences of and responses to the event.
      1.2.3. How does technology have the potential to save lives in hazard zones?
         1.2.3.1. How technological developments can have a positive impact on mitigation (such as building design, prediction, early warning systems) in areas prone to a tectonic hazard of your choice.

2. Changing Climate
   2.1. What evidence is there to suggest climate change is a natural process?
      2.1.1. What evidence is there for climate change?
         2.1.1.1. The pattern of climate change from the beginning of the Quaternary period to the present day.
         2.1.1.2. The range and reliability of evidence relating to climate change including evidence from sea ice positions, ice cores, global temperature data, paintings and diaries.
      2.1.2. Is climate change a natural process?
         2.1.2.1. Outline the causes of natural climate change including the theories of sun spots, volcanic eruptions and Milankovitch cycles.
         2.1.2.2. Investigate the natural greenhouse effect and the impacts that humans have on the atmosphere, including the enhanced greenhouse effect.
      2.1.3. Why is climate change a global issue?
         2.1.3.1. Explore a range of social, economic and environmental impacts of climate change worldwide such as those resulting from sea level rise and extreme weather events. The impacts studied should relate to the 21st century.
         2.1.3.2. Explore a range of social, economic and environmental impacts of climate change within the UK such as the impact on weather patterns, seasonal changes and changes in industry. The impacts studied should relate to the 21st century.

3. Distinctive Landscapes
   3.1. What makes a landscape distinctive?
      3.1.1. What is a landscape?
         3.1.1.1. How the concept of a landscape can be defined, including the differences between built and natural landscapes.
      3.1.2. Where are the physical landscapes of the UK?
         3.1.2.1. Overview of the distribution of upland, lowland and glaciated landscapes in the UK.
         3.1.2.2. Overview of the characteristics of these landscapes which make them distinctive including their geology, climate and human activity.
   3.2. What influences the landscapes of the UK?
      3.2.1. What physical processes shape landscapes?
         3.2.1.1. The geomorphic processes that are involved in shaping landscapes, including weathering (mechanical, chemical, biological), mass movement (sliding, slumping), erosion (abrasion, hydraulic action, attrition, solution), transport (traction, saltation, suspension, solution), deposition.
         3.2.1.2. The formation of coastal landforms including headlands, bays, cave, arch, stack, beach and spit.
         3.2.1.3. The formation of river landforms including waterfall, gorge, v-shaped valley, floodplain, levee, meander, ox-bow lake.
      3.2.2. What are the characteristics of your chosen landscapes?
         3.2.2.1. Case study of two landscapes in the UK, one coastal landscape and one river basin, to include the study of: its landforms created by geomorphic processes, the geomorphic processes operating at different scales and how they are influenced by geology and climate, how human activity, including management, works in combination with geomorphic processes to impact the landscape.

4. Sustaining Ecosystems
   4.1. Why are natural ecosystems important?
      4.1.1. What are ecosystems?
         4.1.1.1. Understand the concept of an ecosystem as being the interdependence of climate, soil, water, plants and animals.
         4.1.1.2. Outline the global distribution of polar regions, coral reefs, grasslands, temperate forests, tropical forests and hot deserts.
         4.1.1.3. Overview of the climate, flora and fauna within these ecosystems.
   4.2. Why should tropical rainforests matter to us?
      4.2.1. What biodiversity exists in tropical rainforests?
         4.2.1.1. The distinctive characteristics of a tropical rainforest ecosystem, including the climate, nutrient cycle, soil profile and water cycle.
         4.2.1.2. The interdependence of climate, soil, water, plants, animals and human activity in tropical rainforests.
      4.2.2. Why are tropical rainforests being ‘exploited’ and how can this be managed sustainably?
         4.2.2.1. Explore the value of tropical rainforests through the study of their goods and services.
         4.2.2.2. Human impacts in the tropical rainforest from activities such as logging, mineral extraction, agriculture and tourism.
         4.2.2.3. A case study to illustrate attempts to sustainably manage an area of tropical rainforest, such as ecotourism, community programmes, biosphere reserves and sustainable forestry, at a local or regional scale.
   4.3. Is there more to polar environments than ice?
      4.3.1. What is it like in Antarctica and the Arctic?
         4.3.1.1. Outline the distinctive characteristics of Antarctica and the Arctic, including climate, features of the land and sea, flora and fauna.
         4.3.1.2. The interdependence of climate, soil, water, plants, animals and human activity in either the Antarctic or the Arctic polar region.
         4.3.1.3. Explore a range of impacts of human activity on either the Antarctic or the Arctic ecosystems, such as scientific research, indigenous people, tourism, fishing, whaling and mineral exploitation.
      4.3.2. How are humans seeking a sustainable solution for polar environments?
         4.3.2.1. A case study to examine one small-scale example of sustainable management in either the Antarctic or the Arctic such as sustainable tourism, conservation and whaling.
         4.3.2.2. A case study to examine one global example of sustainable management in either the Antarctic or the Arctic by investigating global actions such as Earth Summits or the Antarctic Treaty.
