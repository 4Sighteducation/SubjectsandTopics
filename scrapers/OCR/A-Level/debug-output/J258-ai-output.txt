1.1 Chapter C1: Air and water
1.1.1 C1.1 How has the Earth’s atmosphere changed over time, and why?
1.1.1.1 Recall and explain the main features of the particle model in terms of the states of matter and change of state, distinguishing between physical and chemical changes and recognize that the particles themselves do not have the same properties as the bulk substances.
1.1.1.2 Explain the limitations of the particle model in relation to changes of state when particles are represented by inelastic spheres.
1.1.1.3 Use ideas about energy transfers and the relative strength of forces between particles to explain the different temperatures at which changes of state occur.
1.1.1.4 Use data to predict states of substances under given conditions.
1.1.1.5 Interpret evidence for how it is thought the atmosphere was originally formed.
1.1.1.6 Describe how it is thought an oxygen-rich atmosphere developed over time.
1.1.1.7 Describe the major sources of carbon monoxide and particulates (incomplete combustion), sulfur dioxide (combustion of sulfur impurities in fuels), oxides of nitrogen (oxidation of nitrogen at high temperatures and further oxidation in the air).
1.1.1.8 Explain the problems caused by increased amounts of these substances and describe approaches to decreasing the emissions of these substances into the atmosphere including the use of catalytic converters, low sulfur petrol, and gas scrubbers to decrease emissions.
1.1.1.9 Use chemical symbols to write the formulae of elements and simple covalent compounds.
1.1.1.10 Use the names and symbols of common elements and compounds and the principle of conservation of mass to write formulae and balanced chemical equations.
1.1.1.11 Use arithmetic computations and ratios when balancing equations.
1.1.1.12 Describe tests to identify oxygen, hydrogen, and carbon dioxide.
1.1.1.13 Explain oxidation in terms of gain of oxygen.
1.1.2 C1.2 Why are there temperature changes in chemical reactions?
1.1.2.1 Distinguish between endothermic and exothermic reactions on the basis of the temperature change of the surroundings.
1.1.2.2 Draw and label a reaction profile for an exothermic and an endothermic reaction, identifying activation energy.
1.1.2.3 Explain activation energy as the energy needed for a reaction to occur.
1.1.2.4 Interpret charts and graphs when dealing with reaction profiles.
1.1.2.5 Calculate energy changes in a chemical reaction by considering bond breaking and bond making energies.
1.1.2.6 Carry out arithmetic computations when calculating energy changes.
1.1.2.7 Describe how you would investigate a chemical reaction to determine whether it is endothermic or exothermic.
1.1.3 C1.3 What is the evidence for climate change, why is it occurring?
1.1.3.1 Describe the greenhouse effect in terms of the interaction of radiation with matter.
1.1.3.2 Evaluate the evidence for additional anthropogenic causes of climate change, including the correlation between change in atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration and the consumption of fossil fuels, and describe the uncertainties in the evidence base.
1.1.3.3 Describe the potential effects of increased levels of carbon dioxide and methane on the Earth’s climate, including where crops can be grown, extreme weather patterns, melting of polar ice, and flooding of low land.
1.1.3.4 Describe how the effects of increased levels of carbon dioxide and methane may be mitigated, including consideration of scale, risk, and environmental implications.
1.1.3.5 Extract and interpret information from charts, graphs, and tables related to climate change.
1.1.3.6 Use orders of magnitude to evaluate the significance of data.
1.1.4 C1.4 How can scientists help improve the supply of potable water?
1.1.4.1 Describe the principal methods for increasing the availability of potable water, in terms of the separation techniques used, including the ease of treating waste, ground, and salt water including filtration and membrane filtration; aeration, use of bacteria; chlorination and distillation (for salt water).
1.1.4.2 Describe a test to identify chlorine (using blue litmus paper).

1.2 Chapter C2: Chemical patterns
1.2.1 C2.1 How have our ideas about atoms developed over time?
1.2.1.1 Describe how and why the atomic model has changed over time to include the main ideas of Dalton, Thomson, Rutherford, and Bohr.
1.2.1.2 Describe the atom as a positively charged nucleus surrounded by negatively charged electrons, with the nuclear radius much smaller than that of the atom and with most of the mass in the nucleus.
1.2.1.3 Recall relative charges and approximate relative masses of protons, neutrons, and electrons.
1.2.1.4 Estimate the size and scale of atoms relative to other particles.
1.2.1.5 Recall the typical size (order of magnitude) of atoms and small molecules.
1.2.1.6 Relate size and scale of atoms to objects in the physical world.
1.2.1.7 Calculate numbers of protons, neutrons, and electrons in atoms, given atomic number and mass number of isotopes or by extracting data from the Periodic Table.
1.2.2 C2.2 What does the Periodic Table tell us about the elements?
1.2.2.1 Explain how the position of an element in the Periodic Table is related to the arrangement of electrons in its atoms and hence to its atomic number.
1.2.2.2 Describe how Mendeleev organized the elements based on their properties and relative atomic masses.
1.2.2.3 Describe how discovery of new elements and the ordering elements by atomic number supports Mendeleev’s decisions to leave gaps and reorder some elements.
1.2.2.4 Describe metals and non-metals and explain the differences between them on the basis of their characteristic physical and chemical properties, including melting point, boiling point, state and appearance, density, formulae of compounds, relative reactivity, and electrical conductivity.
1.2.2.5 Recall the simple properties of Group 1 elements including their reaction with moist air, water, and chlorine.
1.2.2.6 Recall the simple properties of Group 7 elements including their states and colors at room temperature and pressure, their colors as gases, their reactions with Group 1 elements, and their displacement reactions with other metal halides.
1.2.2.7 Predict possible reactions and probable reactivity of elements from their positions in the Periodic Table.
1.2.2.8 Describe experiments to identify the reactivity pattern of Group 7 elements including displacement reactions.
1.2.2.9 Describe experiments to identify the reactivity pattern of Group 1 elements.
1.2.3 C2.3 How do metals and non-metals combine to form compounds?
1.2.3.1 Recall the simple properties of Group 0 including their low melting and boiling points, their state at room temperature and pressure, and their lack of chemical reactivity.
1.2.3.2 Explain how observed simple properties of Groups 1, 7, and 0 depend on the outer shell of electrons of the atoms and predict properties from given trends down the groups.
1.2.3.3 Explain how the reactions of elements are related to the arrangement of electrons in their atoms and hence to their atomic number.
1.2.3.4 Explain how the atomic structure of metals and non-metals relates to their position in the Periodic Table.
1.2.3.5 Describe the nature and arrangement of chemical bonds in ionic compounds.
1.2.3.6 Explain ionic bonding in terms of electrostatic forces and transfer of electrons.
1.2.3.7 Calculate numbers of protons, neutrons, and electrons in atoms and ions, given atomic number and mass number or by using the Periodic Table.
1.2.3.8 Construct dot and cross diagrams for simple ionic substances.
1.2.3.9 Explain how the bulk properties of ionic materials are related to the type of bonds they contain.
1.2.3.10 Use ideas about energy transfers and the relative strength of attraction between ions to explain the melting points of ionic compounds compared to substances with other types of bonding.
1.2.3.11 Describe the limitations of particular representations and models of ions and ionically bonded compounds, including dot and cross diagrams, and 3-D representations.
1.2.3.12 Translate information between diagrammatic and numerical forms and represent three-dimensional shapes in two dimensions and vice versa when looking at chemical structures for ionic compounds.
1.2.4 C2.4 How are equations used to represent chemical reactions?
1.2.4.1 Use chemical symbols to write the formulae of elements and simple covalent and ionic compounds.
1.2.4.2 Use the formulae of common ions to deduce the formula of Group 1 and Group 7 compounds.
1.2.4.3 Use the names and symbols of the first 20 elements, Groups 1, 7, and 0, and other common elements from a supplied Periodic Table to write formulae and balanced chemical equations where appropriate.
1.2.4.4 Describe the physical states of products and reactants using state symbols (s, l, g, and aq).
