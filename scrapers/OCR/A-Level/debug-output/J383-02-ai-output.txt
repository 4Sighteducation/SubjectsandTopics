1. Ecosystems of the Planet
   1.1. Ecosystems consist of interdependent components.
       1.1.1. Ecosystems include abiotic (weather, climate, soil) and biotic (plants, animals, humans) components which are interdependent.
   1.2. Ecosystems have distinct distributions and characteristics.
       1.2.1. Overview of the global distribution of polar regions, coral reefs, grasslands, temperate forests, tropical rainforests, and hot deserts.
       1.2.2. Overview of the climate, plants, and animals within these ecosystems.
   1.3. There are major tropical rainforests in the world.
       1.3.1. The location of the tropical rainforests including the Amazon, Central American, Congo River Basin, Madagascan, South East Asian, and Australasian.
   1.4. There are major coral reefs in the world.
       1.4.1. The location of warm water coral reefs including the Great Barrier Reef, Red Sea Coral Reef, New Caledonia Barrier Reef, the Mesoamerican Barrier Reef, Florida Reef, and Andros Coral Reef.
   1.5. Bio-diverse ecosystems are under threat from human activity.
       1.5.1. The processes that operate within tropical rainforests, including nutrient and water cycles.
       1.5.2. The process of nutrient cycling that operates within coral reefs.
       1.5.3. Two case studies, including one tropical rainforest and one coral reef, to cover:
           1.5.3.1. The interdependence of climate, soil, water, plants, animals, and humans.
           1.5.3.2. Their value to humans and to the planet.
           1.5.3.3. Threats to biodiversity and attempts to mitigate these through sustainable use and management.

2. People of the Planet
   2.1. The world is developing unevenly.
       2.1.1. Social, economic, and environmental definitions of development, including the concept of sustainable development.
       2.1.2. Different development indicators, including GNI per capita, Human Development Index, and Internet Users, and the advantages and disadvantages of these indicators.
       2.1.3. How development indicators illustrate the consequences of uneven development.
       2.1.4. Current patterns of advanced countries (ACs), emerging and developing countries (EDCs), and low-income developing countries (LIDCs).
   2.2. There are many causes of uneven development.
       2.2.1. Outline the reasons for uneven development, including the impact of colonialism on trade and the exploitation of natural resources.
       2.2.2. Different types of aid and their role in both promoting and hindering development.
   2.3. Many factors contribute to a country’s economic development.
       2.3.1. Case study of one LIDC or EDC. This should illustrate its changing economic development, including the influence of and interrelationships between:
           2.3.1.1. The country’s geographical location, and environmental context (landscape, climate, ecosystems, availability, and type of natural resources).
           2.3.1.2. The country’s political development and relationships with other states.
           2.3.1.3. Principal imports and exports and the relative importance of trade.
           2.3.1.4. The role of international investment.
           2.3.1.5. Population and employment structure changes over time.
           2.3.1.6. Social factors, including access to education and healthcare provision.
           2.3.1.7. Technological developments, such as communications technology.
           2.3.1.8. One aid project.
       2.3.2. Using the case study of the LIDC or EDC explore Rostow’s model to determine the country’s path of economic development.
   2.4. The majority of the world’s population now live in urban areas.
       2.4.1. Definition of city, megacity, and world city.
       2.4.2. The distribution of megacities and how this has changed over time.
       2.4.3. How urban growth rates vary in parts of the world with contrasting levels of development.
   2.5. There are causes and consequences of rapid urbanisation in LIDCs.
       2.5.1. Overview of the causes of rapid urbanisation in LIDCs including push and pull migration factors, and natural growth.
       2.5.2. Outline of the social, economic, and environmental consequences of rapid urbanisation in LIDCs.
   2.6. Cities have distinct challenges and ways of life, influenced by its people and culture.
       2.6.1. Case study of one major city in an LIDC or EDC including the influences of:
           2.6.1.1. The city within its region, the country, and the wider world.
           2.6.1.2. Migration (national and international) and its impact on the city’s growth and character.
           2.6.1.3. The ways of life within the city, such as culture, ethnicity, housing, leisure, and consumption.
           2.6.1.4. Contemporary challenges that affect urban change, including housing availability, transport provision, and waste management.
           2.6.1.5. Sustainable strategies to overcome one of the city’s challenges.

3. Environmental threats to our Planet
   3.1. The climate has changed from the start of the Quaternary period.
       3.1.1. Overview of how the climate has changed from the beginning of the Quaternary period to the present day, including ice ages.
       3.1.2. Key periods of warming and cooling since 1000AD, including the medieval warming, Little Ice Age, and modern warming.
       3.1.3. Evidence for climate change over different time periods, including global temperature data, ice cores, tree rings, paintings, and diaries.
   3.2. There are a number of possible causes of climate change.
       3.2.1. Theories of natural causes of climate change including variations in energy from the sun, changes in the Earth’s orbit, and volcanic activity.
       3.2.2. How human activity is responsible for the enhanced greenhouse effect which contributes to global warming.
   3.3. Climate change has consequences.
       3.3.1. Summary of a range of consequences of climate change currently being experienced across the planet.
   3.4. The global circulation of the atmosphere controls weather and climate.
       3.4.1. Distribution of the main climatic regions of the world.
       3.4.2. Outline how the global circulation of the atmosphere is controlled by the movement of air between the poles and the equator.
       3.4.3. How the global circulation of the atmosphere leads to extreme weather conditions (wind, temperature, precipitation) in different parts of the world.
   3.5. Extreme weather conditions cause different natural weather hazards.
       3.5.1. Outline the causes of the extreme weather conditions that are associated with the hazards of tropical storms and drought.
       3.5.2. The distribution and frequency of tropical storms and drought, and whether these have changed over time.
   3.6. Drought can be devastating for people and the environment.
       3.6.1. Case study of one drought event caused by El Niño/La Niña:
           3.6.1.1. How the extreme weather conditions of El Niño/La Niña develop and can lead to drought.
           3.6.1.2. Effects of the drought event on people and the environment.
           3.6.1.3. Ways in which people have adapted to drought in the case study area.
