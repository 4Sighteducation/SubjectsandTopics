1 Component 01: Socialisation, culture and identity
1.1 Section A: Introducing socialisation, culture and identity
1.1.1 1. What is culture?
1.1.1.1 Culture, norms and values
1.1.1.1.1 be able to understand the relative nature of culture, norms and values. Cross-cultural material should be used here.
1.1.1.2 Types of culture:
1.1.1.2.1 subculture
1.1.1.2.2 high culture
1.1.1.2.3 popular culture
1.1.1.2.4 global culture
1.1.1.2.5 consumer culture
1.1.1.3 Cultural diversity
1.1.1.4 Cultural hybridity
1.1.2 2. What is socialisation?
1.1.2.1 Primary and secondary socialisation
1.1.2.1.1 be able to link definitions of primary socialisation and secondary socialisation to relevant agencies of socialisation, understanding that socialisation is a lifelong process.
1.1.2.2 Agencies of socialisation:
1.1.2.2.1 family
1.1.2.2.2 peer group
1.1.2.2.3 media
1.1.2.2.4 religion
1.1.2.2.5 education
1.1.2.2.6 workplace
1.1.2.2.7 understand the link between socialisation and the creation of identities.
1.1.2.3 Nature/nurture debate
1.1.2.3.1 consider the implications of the nature/nurture debate for an understanding of socialisation.
1.1.2.4 Formal agencies of social control:
1.1.2.4.1 police
1.1.2.4.2 law/legal system
1.1.2.4.3 courts
1.1.2.4.4 government
1.1.2.4.5 military
1.1.2.4.6 explore the overlap between formal and informal social control for work, education and religion.
1.1.2.5 Informal agencies of social control:
1.1.2.5.1 family
1.1.2.5.2 peer group/subcultures
1.1.2.5.3 media
1.1.2.5.4 religion
1.1.2.5.5 education
1.1.2.5.6 workplace
1.1.3 3. What is identity?
1.1.3.1 The concept of identity
1.1.3.1.1 understand how identities are created including the influence of agencies of socialisation.
1.1.3.2 Aspects of identity and the associated cultural characteristics:
1.1.3.2.1 ethnicity
1.1.3.2.2 nationality
1.1.3.2.3 gender
1.1.3.2.4 social class
1.1.3.2.5 sexuality
1.1.3.2.6 age
1.1.3.2.7 disability
1.1.3.2.8 understand the different aspects of an individual’s identity, their relative importance to individuals and ways in which they may intersect.
1.1.3.2.9 consider changing identities.
1.1.3.3 Hybrid identities
1.2 Section B Option 1: Families and relationships
1.2.1 How diverse are modern families?
1.2.1.1 The diversity of family and household types in the contemporary UK:
1.2.1.1.1 nuclear families
1.2.1.1.2 extended families
1.2.1.1.3 lone parent families
1.2.1.1.4 reconstituted families
1.2.1.1.5 same-sex families
1.2.1.1.6 non-family households
1.2.1.1.7 also consider newer/emerging types of families and households.
1.2.1.2 Aspects of and reasons for family and household diversity in the contemporary UK, including:
1.2.1.2.1 trends in marriage, divorce and cohabitation
1.2.1.2.2 demographic changes
1.2.1.2.3 birth rate
1.2.1.2.4 family size
1.2.1.2.5 age at marriage
1.2.1.2.6 age of child-bearing
1.2.1.2.7 ageing population
1.2.1.2.8 family diversity in terms of:
1.2.1.2.8.1 social class
1.2.1.2.8.2 ethnicity
1.2.1.2.8.3 sexuality
1.2.1.2.9 have an overview of trends over the last 30 years and consider the key reasons for these changes. Detailed knowledge of statistics on marriage, divorce and demographic changes is not required.
1.2.1.3 The ideology of the nuclear family and the theoretical debates about the role and desirability of the nuclear family in contemporary society:
1.2.1.3.1 functionalism
1.2.1.3.2 New Right
1.2.1.3.3 Marxism
1.2.1.3.4 feminism
1.2.1.3.5 postmodernism
1.2.1.3.6 consider issues of consensus versus conflict, social order and control in relation to theoretical views of the role of the family in contemporary society. A theoretical approach to considering the extent of family diversity should be taken.
1.2.1.4 Debates about the extent of family diversity in the contemporary UK
1.2.2 To what extent are roles and relationships within families and households changing?
1.2.2.1 Roles and relationships between partners and how they are changing, including issues of power
1.2.2.1.1 consider a range of issues including the domestic division of labour and the ‘dark side’ of the family. A theoretical approach should be taken, applying functionalist, Marxist and feminist approaches.
1.2.2.2 Roles and relationships between parents and children and how they are changing, including issues of power
1.2.2.2.1 consider issues such as the growth of the child-centred family and the extension of childhood.
1.2.2.2.2 consider the impact of the ageing population on the relationship between grandparents, parents and children. A theoretical approach should be taken, applying feminist approaches.
1.3 Section B Option 2: Youth subcultures
1.3.1 How and why are youth culture and subcultures formed?
1.3.1.1 Theoretical views of the role and formation of youth culture and subcultures:
1.3.1.1.1 functionalism
1.3.1.1.2 Marxism/neo-Marxism
1.3.1.1.3 feminism
1.3.1.1.4 postmodernism
1.3.1.1.5 use illustrative examples of subcultures to explore how and why youth culture and subcultures are formed.
1.3.1.1.6 consider issues of consensus versus conflict, social order and control in relation to theoretical views of the role and formation of youth culture and subculture.
1.3.1.2 Subcultures as related to:
1.3.1.2.1 social class
1.3.1.2.2 gender
1.3.1.2.3 ethnicity
1.3.1.2.4 hybridity
1.3.2 Why do young people participate in deviant subcultures?
1.3.2.1 Deviant subcultures:
1.3.2.1.1 delinquent subcultures
1.3.2.1.2 criminal subcultures
1.3.2.1.3 spectacular youth subcultures
1.3.2.1.4 anti-school subcultures
1.3.2.1.5 gangs
1.3.2.1.6 consider newer/emerging types of deviant subcultures.
1.3.2.2 Patterns and trends in youth deviance related to:
1.3.2.2.1 social class
1.3.2.2.2 gender
1.3.2.2.3 ethnicity
1.3.2.2.4 consider patterns and trends (within the last 30 years) of youth deviance based on the evidence presented by official statistics.
1.3.2.3 Explanations for young people participating in deviant subcultures:
1.3.2.3.1 functionalism/New Right
1.3.2.3.2 Marxism/neo-Marxism
1.3.2.3.3 interactionism
1.3.2.3.4 culture and identity
1.3.2.3.5 consider both theoretical and identity based explanations, which could include issues of ethnic identity and gender.
1.3.2.4 The media and youth deviance:
1.3.2.4.1 deviance amplification
1.3.2.4.2 folk devils
1.3.2.4.3 moral panics
1.4 Section B Option 3: Media
1.4.1 How are different social groups represented in the media?
1.4.1.1 Evidence of representations in the media and how far these are changing in relation to:
1.4.1.1.1 ethnicity (majority and minority ethnic groups)
1.4.1.1.2 gender (masculinity and femininity)
1.4.1.1.3 social class (middle, working, upper, under)
1.4.1.1.4 age (young and old)
1.4.1.1.5 be able to discuss representation of a range of ethnicities. For example, learners could consider UK nationalities (Irish, Scottish, Welsh, English) and also newer immigrant groups as well as more established minority ethnic groups.
1.4.1.2 Theoretical views of media representations:
1.4.1.2.1 Marxism
1.4.1.2.2 neo-Marxism
1.4.1.2.3 pluralism
1.4.1.2.4 feminism
1.4.1.2.5 postmodernism
1.4.1.2.6 understand explanations for these representations, and any changing representations, utilising theoretical perspectives as appropriate.
1.4.1.2.7 consider issues of consensus versus conflict, social order and control in relation to theoretical views of media representations.
1.4.1.2.8 use postmodernism as a critique of other theoretical views.
1.4.2 What effect do the media have on audiences?
1.4.2.1 Theoretical views of media effects:
1.4.2.1.1 direct
1.4.2.1.2 indirect
1.4.2.1.3 active audience
1.4.2.1.4 understand a range of theories on the effects of the media on the audience, including: hypodermic syringe; two-step flow; cultural effects; uses and gratifications.
1.4.2.2 The role of the media in deviance amplification and the creation of moral panics
