1 Foundation Tier
1.1 B1: You and your genes
1.1.1 B1.1 What is the genome and what does it do?
1.1.1.1 Explain how the nucleus and genetic material of eukaryotic cells (plants and animals) and the genetic material, including plasmids, of prokaryotic cells are related to cell functions.
1.1.1.2 Describe how to use a light microscope to observe a variety of plant and animal cells.
1.1.1.3 Describe the genome as the entire genetic material of an organism.
1.1.1.4 Describe DNA as a polymer made up of nucleotides, forming two strands in a double helix.
1.1.1.5 Describe simply how the genome and its interaction with the environment influence the development of the phenotype of an organism, including the idea that most characteristics depend on instructions in the genome and are modified by interaction of the organism with its environment.
1.1.1.6 Explain the terms chromosome, gene, allele, variant, genotype and phenotype.
1.1.1.7 Explain the importance of amino acids in the synthesis of proteins, including the genome as instructions for the polymerisation of amino acids to make proteins.
1.1.2 B1.2 How is genetic information inherited?
1.1.2.1 Explain the terms gamete, homozygous, heterozygous, dominant and recessive.
1.1.2.2 Explain single gene inheritance, including dominant and recessive alleles and use of genetic diagrams.
1.1.2.3 Predict the results of single gene crosses.
1.1.2.4 Use direct proportions and simple ratios in genetic crosses.
1.1.2.5 Use the concept of probability in predicting the outcome of genetic crosses.
1.1.2.6 Recall that most phenotypic features are the result of multiple genes rather than single gene inheritance.
1.1.2.7 Describe sex determination in humans.
1.1.3 B1.3 How can and should gene technology be used?
1.1.3.1 Discuss the potential importance for medicine of our increasing understanding of the human genome, including the discovery of alleles associated with genetic diseases and the genetic testing of individuals to inform family planning and healthcare.
1.1.3.2 Describe genetic engineering as a process which involves modifying the genome of an organism to introduce desirable characteristics.
1.1.3.3 Describe the main steps in the process of genetic engineering.
1.1.3.4 Explain some of the possible benefits and risks, including practical and ethical considerations, of using gene technology in modern agriculture and medicine.

1.3 B3: Living together – food and ecosystems
1.3.1 B3.1 What happens during photosynthesis?

1.3.1.5 Use the inverse square law to explain changes in the rate of photosynthesis with distance from a light source.

1.3.2.3 Explain how the partially-permeable cell membranes of plant cells and prokaryotic cells are related to diffusion, osmosis and active transport.
1.3.2.4 Explain how water and mineral ions are taken up by plants, relating the structure of the root hair cells to their function.
1.3.2.5 Explain how the structure of the xylem and phloem are adapted to their functions in the plant.
1.3.2.6 Describe the processes of transpiration and translocation, including the structure and function of the stomata.

1.3.3.10 Explain the effect of factors such as temperature and water content on rate of decomposition in aerobic and anaerobic environments.
1.3.3.11 Calculate rate changes in the decay of biological material.
