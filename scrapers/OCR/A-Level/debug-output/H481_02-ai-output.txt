1. Human Interactions
   1.1 Changing Spaces; Making Places
      1.1.1 1.a. Places are multi-faceted, shaped by shifting flows and connections which change over time.
         1.1.1.1 Case studies of two contrasting place profiles at a local scale, including:
         1.1.1.2 their demographic, socio-economic, cultural, political, built and natural characteristics that shape their place identity.
         1.1.1.3 their past and present connections that shape the place identity and embed them in regional, national, international and global scales.
         1.1.1.4 how shifting flows of people (such as commuter, migration), resources (such as natural, technology), money and investment (such as EU funding, TNCs) and ideas (such as knowledge economy) have helped shape the demographic, socio-economic and cultural profile of these places over time.
      1.1.2 2.a. People see, experience and understand place in different ways, this can also change over time.
         1.1.2.1 The complexities that exist when trying to define place, including the concept of space versus place.
         1.1.2.2 How and why people perceive places in different ways based on their identity, including age, gender, sexuality, religion and role.
         1.1.2.3 How level of emotional attachment to place can influence people’s behaviour and activities in a place.
         1.1.2.4 How the processes of globalisation and time-space compression can influence our sense of place.
      1.1.3 2.b. Places are represented through a variety of contrasting formal and informal agencies.
         1.1.3.1 How informal representations of a place differ through contrasting media such as TV, film, music, art, photography, literature, graffiti and blogs.
         1.1.3.2 Identify how formal and statistical representations of a place, such as census and geospatial data, contrasts with informal representations.
      1.1.4 3.a. The distribution of resources, wealth and opportunities are not evenly spread within and between places.
         1.1.4.1 The concept of social inequality and how this can be measured through indices such as housing, healthcare, education, employment and access to services.
         1.1.4.2 How and why spatial patterns of social inequalities vary both within and between places.
      1.1.5 3.b. Processes of economic change can create opportunities for some while creating and exacerbating social inequality for others.
         1.1.5.1 The influence of global connections and globalisation in driving structural economic change in places, such as de-industrialisation and the rise of the service industry.
         1.1.5.2 How structural economic change impacts patterns of social opportunities and inequality for people and places.
         1.1.5.3 How cyclical economic change (booms and recessions) has varied impacts on social opportunities and inequality.
         1.1.5.4 The role of government in reducing, reinforcing and creating patterns of social inequality in places through spending or cuts in key services such as availability and accessibility of education, healthcare, infrastructure and community services.
      1.1.6 3.c. Social inequality impacts people and places in different ways.
         1.1.6.1 Case studies of two contrasting places to illustrate:
         1.1.6.2 the types of evidence of social inequality that can be found there such as housing, environmental quality, crime rates, digital divide.
         1.1.6.3 the range of factors that influence people’s social inequality such as income, gender, age, health, personal mobility, ethnicity and education.
         1.1.6.4 how social inequality impacts upon people’s daily lives in different ways.
      1.1.7 4.a. Places are influenced by a range of players operating at different scales.
         1.1.7.1 The role of players in driving economic change, including at least one of local and national government, MNCs or international institutions.
         1.1.7.2 Case study of one country or region that has been impacted by structural economic change, including:
         1.1.7.3 socio-economic, demographic, cultural and environmental characteristics of the place before the economic change.
         1.1.7.4 the economic change/changes that took place and the role of players involved in driving the change.
         1.1.7.5 socio-economic, demographic, cultural and environmental impacts on people and place.
      1.1.8 5.a. Place is produced in a variety of ways at different scales.
         1.1.8.1 The concept of placemaking and how governments and organisations attempt to present places to the wider world to attract inward investment and regeneration.
         1.1.8.2 How architects and planners attempt to create meaningful and authentic places through design, such as places that encourage mixed community use or the 24 hour city.
         1.1.8.3 How local community groups shape the place they live, such as residents associations, heritage associations and social media.
      1.1.9 5.b. The placemaking process of rebranding constructs a different place meaning through reimaging and regeneration.
         1.1.9.1 Why places rebrand through reimaging and regeneration to construct a different place meaning.
         1.1.9.2 How a range of strategies can be used to rebrand places, such as sport, art, heritage, retail, architecture and food. These can be used singularly or in conjunction to change a place meaning.
         1.1.9.3 A range of players and their role in placemaking, including government/EU funding, corporate bodies, not for profit organisations and community groups.
         1.1.9.4 How and why some groups of people contest efforts to rebrand a place.
      1.1.10 5.c. Making a successful place requires planning and design.
         1.1.10.1 Case study of one place that has undergone rebranding, including:
         1.1.10.2 why the place needed to rebrand.
         1.1.10.3 strategy/strategies involved in the rebranding of the place.
         1.1.10.4 the role and influence of a range of players involved in the placemaking.
         1.1.10.5 how the rebranding has altered people’s perception of that place.
         1.1.10.6 the relative success of the rebranding.
   1.2 Global Connections
      1.2.1 Global Systems: Option A – Trade in the Contemporary World
         1.2.1.1 1.a. International trade involves flows of merchandise, services and capital which vary spatially.
            1.2.1.1.1 An understanding of the terms merchandise, services and capital as components of international trade.
            1.2.1.1.2 Current spatial patterns in the direction and components of international trade, including examples of both inter-regional and intra-regional.
         1.2.1.2 1.b. Current patterns of international trade are related to global patterns of socio-economic development.
            1.2.1.2.1 The relationship between patterns of international trade and socio-economic development using national indices, such as ‘value of exports’ and ‘Human Development Index’.
            1.2.1.2.2 How international trade can promote stability, growth and development within and between countries, through flows of people, money, ideas and technology.
            1.2.1.2.3 How international trade causes inequalities, conflicts and injustices for people and places, through flows of people, money, ideas and technology.
         1.2.1.3 2.a. Access to markets are influenced by a multitude of inter-related factors.
            1.2.1.3.1 International trade has increased connectivity due to changes in the 21st century, including:
            1.2.1.3.2 technology, transport and communications have increased connectivity of global supply chains.
            1.2.1.3.3 increasing influence of MNCs in EDCs, including outsourcing.
            1.2.1.3.4 role of regional trading blocs, such as the EU.
            1.2.1.3.5 growth of ‘south-south’ trade, between developing countries.
            1.2.1.3.6 growth of services in the global economy.
            1.2.1.3.7 increasing labour mobility and new international division of labour.
         1.2.1.4 2.b. There is interdependence between countries and their trading partners.
            1.2.1.4.1 Case study of one EDC to illustrate:
            1.2.1.4.2 direction and components of its current international trade patterns.
            1.2.1.4.3 changes in its international trade patterns over time.
            1.2.1.4.4 economic, political, social and environmental interdependence with trading partners.
            1.2.1.4.5 impacts of trade on the EDC, including economic development, political stability and social equality.
         1.2.1.5 3.a. International trade creates opportunities and challenges which reflect unequal power relations between countries.
            1.2.1.5.1 Case study of one AC to show how core economies have a strong influence and drive change in the global trade system to their own advantage. Illustrate through economic, political and social factors to explain:
            1.2.1.5.2 its advantages for trade, including patterns, partners, negotiations and agreements.
            1.2.1.5.3 opportunities, such as sustained economic growth.
            1.2.1.5.4 challenges, such as trade deficit.
            1.2.1.5.5 Case study of one LIDC to show how peripheral economies exert limited influence and can only respond to change in the global trade system. Illustrate this through economic, political and social factors to explain:
            1.2.1.5.6 trade components, including patterns, partners, negotiations and agreements.
            1.2.1.5.7 why it has limited access to global markets.
            1.2.1.5.8 opportunities, such as diversification of economic activity.
            1.2.1.5.9 challenges, such as political instability.
      1.2.2 Global Systems: Option B – Global Migration
         1.2.2.1 1.a. Global migration involves dynamic flows of people between countries, regions and continents.
            1.2.2.1.1 Current spatial patterns in the numbers, composition and direction of international migrant flows, including examples of both inter-regional and intra-regional.
         1.2.2.2 1.b. Current patterns of international migration are related to global patterns of socio-economic development.
            1.2.2.2.1 The relationship between patterns of international migration and socio-economic development, using national indices such as ‘value of migrant remittances’ and ‘Human Development Index’.
            1.2.2.2.2 How global migration can promote stability, growth and development within and between countries through flows of people, money, ideas and technology.
            1.2.2.2.3 How global migration causes inequalities, conflicts and injustices for people and places through flows of people, money, ideas and technology.
         1.2.2.3 2.a. Global migration patterns are influenced by a multitude of inter-related factors.
            1.2.2.3.1 Changes in the 21st century have increased the complexity of global migration, including:
            1.2.2.3.2 economic globalisation leading to the emergence of new source areas and host destinations.
            1.2.2.3.3 high concentration of young workers and female migrants.
            1.2.2.3.4 flows in South-South corridors are now equal in magnitude to those in South-North corridors.
            1.2.2.3.5 conflict and persecution have increased numbers of refugees.
            1.2.2.3.6 changes in national immigration and emigration policies.
            1.2.2.3.7 development of distinct corridors of bi-lateral flows.
         1.2.2.4 2.b. Corridors of migrant flows create interdependence between countries.
            1.2.2.4.1 Case study of one EDC to illustrate:
            1.2.2.4.2 current patterns of immigration and emigration.
            1.2.2.4.3 changes in immigration and emigration over time.
            1.2.2.4.4 economic, political, social and environmental interdependence with countries connected to the EDC by migrant flows.
            1.2.2.4.5 the impact of migration on the EDCs economic development, political stability and social equality.
         1.2.2.5 3.a. Global migration creates opportunities and challenges which reflect the unequal power relations between countries.
            1.2.2.5.1 Case study of one AC to show how it influences and drives change in the global migration system. Illustrate economic, political and social factors which explain:
            1.2.2.5.2 patterns of emigration and immigration, migration policies, and interdependence with countries linked to it by migration.
            1.2.2.5.3 opportunities, such as labour supply.
            1.2.2.5.4 challenges, such as border issues.
            1.2.2.5.5 Case study of one LIDC to show how it has limited influence over and restricted response to the global migration system. Illustrate economic, political and social factors which explain:
            1.2.2.5.6 patterns of emigration and immigration, migration policies, and interdependence with countries linked to it by migration.
            1.2.2.5.7 opportunities, such as migrant remittances.
            1.2.2.5.8 challenges, such as loss of skilled workers.
